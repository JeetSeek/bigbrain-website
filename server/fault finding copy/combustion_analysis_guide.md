# Combustion Analysis and Testing Guide

This comprehensive guide covers diagnostic procedures using combustion analysis equipment, including CO/CO₂ ratio interpretation, combustion efficiency testing, flue gas analyzer use, and burner pressure testing.

## PART 1: UNDERSTANDING COMBUSTION ANALYSIS

### Principles of Combustion Analysis

**1. Complete Combustion Process**
- **Fuel + Oxygen → CO₂ + H₂O + Heat**
- Perfect combustion produces only carbon dioxide and water
- Maximum heat extraction occurs with optimal air/fuel ratio
- Theoretical air requirement varies by fuel type
- Excess air needed for real-world complete combustion

**2. Key Combustion Parameters**
- **Air/Fuel Ratio**: Balance between combustion air and fuel
- **Excess Air**: Extra air beyond stoichiometric requirement
- **CO/CO₂ Ratio**: Indicator of combustion quality
- **Flue Gas Temperature**: Efficiency and heat transfer indicator
- **Oxygen Content**: Measures excess air level
- **Carbon Monoxide**: Indicator of incomplete combustion

**3. Impact on System Performance**
- Efficiency directly related to combustion quality
- Safety dependent on proper combustion
- Longevity of components affected by combustion byproducts
- Environmental impact of emissions
- Fuel consumption rates

### Combustion Testing Equipment

**1. Electronic Flue Gas Analyzers**

- Digital displays with calculated parameters

- Regular calibration requirements

**2. Key Components**

- Temperature sensors (flue and ambient)

**3. Analyzer Capabilities**
- Direct measurement of O₂ and CO

**4. Care and Maintenance**
- Regular sensor calibration

- Battery management
- Sensor replacement schedules
- Storage considerations

## PART 2: CO/CO₂ RATIO INTERPRETATION

### Understanding CO/CO₂ Ratio

**Definition and Calculation:**
- Ratio of carbon monoxide to carbon dioxide in flue gas
- Expressed as parts per million CO per % CO₂
- Calculated: CO (ppm) ÷ CO₂ (%)
- Indicates combustion quality independent of dilution
- More reliable than absolute CO measurements

**Significance:**
- Lower ratio indicates better combustion
- Unaffected by excess air dilution
- Direct indicator of burner performance
- Key safety parameter
- Used in manufacturer specifications

**Reference Values:**
- Natural gas appliances: < 0.004 (excellent), 0.004-0.008 (acceptable)
- LPG appliances: < 0.003 (excellent), 0.003-0.006 (acceptable)
- Oil appliances: < 0.01 (excellent), 0.01-0.02 (acceptable)
- Values above these ranges indicate combustion issues

**Diagnostic Procedures:**

1. **Ratio Measurement**
   - Ensure analyzer is properly calibrated
   - Insert probe into correct test point
   - Allow readings to stabilize (minimum 1 minute)
   - Record CO, CO₂, and calculated ratio
   - Test at multiple firing rates if modulating
   - Compare with manufacturer's specifications

2. **Trend Analysis**
   - Compare with previous test results
   - Note changes over time
   - Check for pattern changes at different firing rates
   - Observe stability of reading over extended operation
   - Record alongside other performance parameters

3. **Comparative Testing**
   - Test similar appliances for comparison
   - Compare with benchmark data
   - Evaluate against manufacturer's commissioning data
   - Check industry standards for appliance type
   - Reference gas safety guidelines

**Common Issues and Interpretations:**

1. **High CO/CO₂ Ratio**
   - Symptoms: Ratio above acceptable range
   - Possible Causes:
     * Insufficient primary air
     * Burner blockage or damage
     * Incorrect gas pressure
     * Heat exchanger fouling
     * Flue restriction
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Check and adjust primary air
     * Clean burner components
     * Verify gas pressure
     * Inspect heat exchanger
     * Check flue condition

2. **Rising CO/CO₂ Ratio Over Time**
   - Symptoms: Increasing ratio in successive tests
   - Possible Causes:
     * Progressive burner fouling
     * Gradual component deterioration
     * Developing heat exchanger issues
     * Increasing flue restriction
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Complete burner service
     * Restore original specifications
     * Preventative component replacement
     * Document trend for future reference

3. **Unstable CO/CO₂ Ratio**
   - Symptoms: Fluctuating ratio during testing
   - Possible Causes:
     * Unstable gas pressure
     * Intermittent fan issues
     * Partial blockages
     * Control system problems
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Check gas pressure stability
     * Verify fan operation
     * Inspect for intermittent restrictions
     * Test control system function

### Advanced Interpretation Techniques

**Multi-Point Analysis:**

## PART 3: COMBUSTION EFFICIENCY TESTING

### Efficiency Calculation Principles

**Combustion Efficiency Components:**
- Chemical energy conversion completeness
- Heat transfer effectiveness
- Stack loss measurement
- Excess air optimization
- Return condensation credit (condensing appliances)

**Calculation Method:**
- 100% minus stack losses
- Accounts for sensible heat loss
- Considers latent heat loss
- Factors in unburned fuel
- Specific to fuel type

**Influencing Factors:**
- Excess air level
- Fuel composition
- Flue gas temperature
- Ambient temperature
- Burner design and condition
- Heat exchanger effectiveness
- Condensing capability

**Diagnostic Procedures:**

1. **Pre-Test Preparation**
   - Ensure appliance at normal operating temperature
   - Verify stable operating conditions
   - Record ambient temperature
   - Confirm analyzer calibration
   - Select correct fuel type in analyzer
   - Check recommended test points

2. **Efficiency Measurement**
   - Insert probe into correct sampling point
   - Verify proper probe positioning
   - Allow readings to stabilize
   - Record net and gross efficiency
   - Test at multiple firing rates if applicable
   - Document all test parameters

3. **Comparative Analysis**
   - Check against manufacturer's specification
   - Compare with previous test results
   - Evaluate against benchmark data
   - Consider seasonal variations
   - Reference regulatory minimum requirements

**Common Efficiency Issues:**

1. **Low Combustion Efficiency**
   - Symptoms: Below manufacturer specification or regulatory minimum
   - Possible Causes:
     * Excessive excess air
     * High flue gas temperature
     * Poor heat transfer
     * Incorrect burner setup
     * Component deterioration
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Optimize air/fuel ratio
     * Check for heat exchanger fouling
     * Verify correct gas pressure
     * Adjust burner settings
     * Clean heat transfer surfaces

2. **Declining Efficiency Trend**
   - Symptoms: Efficiency lower than previous tests
   - Possible Causes:
     * Progressive fouling
     * Developing component issues
     * Calibration drift
     * Control system deterioration
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Complete system cleaning
     * Restore to original specifications
     * Recalibrate controls
     * Document rate of decline

3. **Efficiency Below Condensing Threshold**
   - Symptoms: Condensing boiler operating in non-condensing mode
   - Possible Causes:
     * Flow temperature too high
     * Return temperature above dew point
     * Control strategy issues
     * System design problems
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Reduce flow temperature
     * Check hydraulic design
     * Verify weather compensation
     * Examine control strategy

### Optimizing Combustion Efficiency

**Excess Air Management:**
- Balance between safety and efficiency
- Too low: incomplete combustion, CO production
- Too high: excessive heat loss up flue
- Optimal: 20-30% excess air for most appliances
- Regular adjustment to maintain optimal setting

**Heat Transfer Enhancement:**
- Clean heat exchanger surfaces
- Maintain optimal flow rates
- Proper burner flame pattern
- Correct baffles and turbulators
- Appropriate flue draft

**Control Strategy Improvement:**
- Modulating operation optimization
- Weather-based compensation
- Return temperature management
- Firing rate optimization
- Anti-cycling strategies

**System Design Considerations:**
- Hydraulic separation for optimal flow
- Proper pump sizing and configuration
- Correct balancing for flow distribution
- Appropriate return temperature management
- Buffer tank integration where beneficial

## PART 4: FLUE GAS ANALYZER USE AND INTERPRETATION

### Analyzer Setup and Operation

**Pre-Use Preparation:**
- Battery check and charging
- Zero calibration in clean air
- Filter and water trap inspection
- Probe condition verification
- Sensor warm-up period
- Correct fuel selection

**Sampling Technique:**
- Proper test point location
- Correct probe insertion depth
- Center of flue gas stream positioning
- Adequate stabilization time
- Multiple sampling points for large flues
- Avoiding air infiltration

**Measurement Procedure:**
- Wait for temperature stabilization
- Observe readings until stable
- Record all parameter values
- Check for consistency between readings
- Note operating conditions
- Preserve sample data

**Common Operational Issues:**
- Water trap flooding
- Filter clogging
- Battery depletion during testing
- Sensor saturation
- Probe contamination
- Connection leaks

**Diagnostic Procedures:**

1. **Comprehensive Gas Analysis**
   - Measure O₂, CO, CO₂, NO, NO₂
   - Calculate air/fuel ratio
   - Determine excess air percentage
   - Assess combustion efficiency
   - Record flue and ambient temperatures
   - Calculate temperature differential

2. **Data Interpretation**
   - Compare against manufacturer's specifications
   - Check regulatory compliance
   - Evaluate historical trends
   - Assess overall combustion quality
   - Identify specific component issues
   - Determine adjustment requirements

3. **Problem Identification**
   - High CO: Incomplete combustion
   - High O₂: Excessive excess air
   - High flue temperature: Poor heat transfer
   - Fluctuating readings: Unstable combustion
   - Rising CO with falling O₂: Primary air issues
   - High NO/NOx: Excessive flame temperature

**Common Analyzer Readings and Interpretations:**

1. **Optimal Combustion Profile**
   - Natural Gas:
     * O₂: 3-5%
     * CO₂: 8.5-10.5%
     * CO: <100 ppm
     * Efficiency: 87-95% (non-condensing), 90-98% (condensing)
     * Flue temperature: 150-250°C (non-condensing), 40-75°C (condensing)

2. **Oxygen-Rich Combustion**
   - Symptoms: O₂ >7%, CO₂ <8%, lower efficiency
   - Possible Causes:
     * Excess primary air
     * Oversized fan or draft
     * Air leaks in combustion chamber
     * Improper burner adjustment
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Reduce air supply
     * Check for air leaks
     * Adjust burner according to specifications
     * Verify draft control operation

3. **Fuel-Rich Combustion**
   - Symptoms: O₂ <2%, high CO, sooting
   - Possible Causes:
     * Insufficient primary air
     * Blocked air passages
     * Excessive gas pressure
     * Damaged burner components
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Increase primary air
     * Clean air passages
     * Verify correct gas pressure
     * Inspect and repair burner

## PART 5: BURNER PRESSURE AND GAS RATE TESTING

### Understanding Gas Pressure Parameters

**Critical Pressure Points:**
- Gas inlet pressure (before gas valve)
- Burner pressure (after gas valve)
- Manifold pressure (at burner injectors)
- Differential pressure (across components)
- Operating pressure vs. standing pressure

**Pressure Requirements:**
- Natural gas appliance burner pressure: Typically 8-17 mbar
- LPG appliance burner pressure: Typically 25-37 mbar
- Inlet pressure (natural gas): 19-23 mbar
- Inlet pressure (LPG): 37 mbar (propane) or 28 mbar (butane)
- Maximum allowable drop under operation: 2 mbar

**Influence on Combustion:**
- Direct relationship to gas flow rate
- Affects primary air entrainment
- Impacts flame stability
- Determines heat input rate
- Critical for proper modulation

**Diagnostic Procedures:**

1. **Pressure Measurement Setup**
   - Use properly calibrated manometer
   - Connect to correct test points
   - Ensure gas-tight connections
   - Allow for system stabilization
   - Observe dynamic vs. static readings

2. **Multi-Point Testing**
   - Standing pressure (system off)
   - Working pressure (system running)
   - Maximum rate pressure
   - Minimum rate pressure (if modulating)
   - Pressure drop on ignition
   - Recovery time after shutdown

3. **System-Wide Pressure Analysis**
   - Gas meter outlet pressure
   - Pressure at appliance inlet
   - Pressure drop across system
   - Impact of multiple appliance operation
   - Effect of other gas loads in property

**Common Pressure Issues:**

1. **Low Inlet Pressure**
   - Symptoms: Below 19 mbar NG, below 35 mbar LPG
   - Possible Causes:
     * Undersized gas supply pipe
     * Partially blocked filter
     * Regulator failure
     * Multiple appliance demand
     * Supply issue from network
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Check and clean filters
     * Verify regulator operation
     * Assess pipe sizing adequacy
     * Test with other appliances off
     * Check meter and ECV condition

2. **Incorrect Burner Pressure**
   - Symptoms: Outside manufacturer's specified range
   - Possible Causes:
     * Incorrect adjustment
     * Gas valve issue
     * Control system fault
     * Modulation problems
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Adjust to correct specification
     * Clean/replace gas valve if faulty
     * Check control signals to valve
     * Verify valve operation across range

3. **Pressure Drop During Operation**
   - Symptoms: Significant drop when burner ignites
   - Possible Causes:
     * Undersized pipework
     * Partially blocked gas line
     * Inadequate regulator capacity
     * Shared supply limitations
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Verify pipe sizing calculations
     * Clean or replace filters
     * Check for restrictions
     * Consider supply upgrade if needed

### Gas Rate Testing

**Importance of Gas Rate:**
- Determines actual heat input
- Verifies proper appliance sizing
- Confirms burner pressure correlation
- Checks meter accuracy
- Verifies manufacturer specifications

**Measurement Methods:**
- Timed meter test (most common)
- Flow meter measurement
- Calculated from combustion parameters
- Differential pressure conversion
- Electronic flow measurement

**Diagnostic Procedures:**

1. **Timed Meter Test**
   - Ensure only test appliance operating
   - Operate appliance at full rate
   - Time consumption of fixed volume (typically 1 ft³)
   - Convert to kW using appropriate formula
   - Compare with appliance data plate

2. **Heat Input Calculation**
   - Calculate based on gas type and consumption
   - Natural gas: 1 ft³ ≈ 10.96 kWh
   - Propane: 1 ft³ ≈ 26.8 kWh
   - Butane: 1 ft³ ≈ 33.5 kWh
   - Adjust for local gas calorific value
   - Compare with boiler rating

3. **Gas Rate Adjustment**
   - Verify relationship between pressure and rate
   - Check burner injector condition and size
   - Adjust gas valve if within permitted range
   - Document all adjustments made
   - Retest to confirm correct rate

**Common Gas Rate Issues:**

1. **Excessive Gas Rate**
   - Symptoms: Higher than specified input, high flue temperature
   - Possible Causes:
     * Incorrect pressure setting
     * Oversized injectors
     * Incorrect gas valve adjustment
     * Control system fault
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Adjust to correct pressure
     * Verify correct injector size
     * Check valve modulation operation
     * Ensure gas valve properly responding to controls

2. **Insufficient Gas Rate**
   - Symptoms: Below specified input, performance issues
   - Possible Causes:
     * Low gas pressure
     * Partially blocked injectors
     * Restrictions in gas supply
     * Incorrect pressure setting
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Clean injectors
     * Check for supply restrictions
     * Verify correct pressure settings
     * Test supply adequacy

3. **Unstable Gas Rate**
   - Symptoms: Fluctuating input, flame stability issues
   - Possible Causes:
     * Pressure regulator problems
     * Competing gas loads
     * Control system hunting
     * Gas valve dysfunction
   - Solution Approaches:
     * Check regulator operation
     * Test with other appliances inactive
     * Evaluate control system function
     * Inspect gas valve for erratic behavior

### Modulating Burner Testing

**Modulation Characteristics:**
- Turndown ratio capabilities
- Linear vs. non-linear modulation curves
- Minimum stable firing rate
- Control signal correlation
- Response time and stability

**Test Procedure:**
1. Test at maximum rate
2. Verify minimum rate operation
3. Check several intermediate points
4. Assess smooth transition between rates
5. Confirm control signal properly translated to gas rate
6. Measure stability at each test point

**Common Modulation Issues:**
- Limited modulation range
- Unstable operation at low fire
- Non-responsive to control signals
- Hunting or oscillation
- Poor correlation between signal and actual rate

**Adjustment Techniques:**
- Gas valve characterization
- Minimum rate setting optimization
- Control signal calibration
- Response damping if needed
- Rate limit setting for stability

### Troubleshooting Common Combustion Problems

| Symptom | Possible Causes | Test Method | Solution Approach |
|---------|----------------|------------|-------------------|
| High CO | Insufficient air, blocked heat exchanger | Full combustion analysis | Clean heat exchanger, increase primary air |
| Fluctuating readings | Unstable gas pressure, fan issues | Extended monitoring | Check gas supply, fan operation |
| Low efficiency | Excess air, high flue temperature | Efficiency calculation | Optimize air/fuel ratio, clean heat transfer surfaces |
| Sooting | Fuel-rich mixture, poor atomization (oil) | Visual + gas analysis | Correct air/fuel ratio, service burner |
| Condensing boiler not condensing | High return temp, improper setup | Flow/return temp check | Reduce flow temp, check system design |
| Yellow flame (NG) | Insufficient primary air, burner blockage | Visual + CO/CO₂ | Clean burner, adjust air supply |
| Unstable flame | Incorrect pressure, burner issues | Pressure test + visual | Correct pressure, clean/repair burner |
